Warm-Up Protocol

Use this before every session, regardless of split or which day you're training. Do not skip — cold muscles underperform and carry higher injury risk.

1. General warm-up (5 min): Light cardio — rowing machine, bike, or brisk walk on an incline. Raises core temperature and primes the whole body before lifting.
2. Joint mobility (3-5 min): Arm circles, leg swings, hip circles, shoulder rotations. Move every major joint you're about to train through its full range a few times.
3. Movement-specific warm-up sets: Before your first compound lift of the session (squat, bench, deadlift, row), do 2-3 progressively heavier warm-up sets at low reps (5-8) before your working weight. For example, if your working set is 60kg — try empty bar/light weight × 8, then roughly half your working weight × 5, then a set close to working weight × 3, then begin your programmed sets.

HYPERTROPHY PROGRAMME

Choose Your Training Split

Pick how many days per week you can train. This determines which sessions you'll follow throughout the 6 weeks. You can change this at any time.

3 Days / Week
Full Body
Every major muscle group trained 3× per week. Best for beginners and intermediates with limited training days.
4 Days / Week
Upper / Lower
Upper body and lower body alternating. Each muscle group trained twice per week with higher per-session volume.
5 Days / Week
PPL + Upper / Lower
Push / Pull / Legs plus two additional upper and lower days. Maximum frequency and volume for advanced trainees.
How tempo works in this programme: Research shows tempo manipulation has little direct effect on muscle growth compared to total volume and training close to failure. Most exercises use a natural, controlled pace. Where a specific tempo IS listed (e.g. 3-1-1), it serves a purpose — reinforcing the mind-muscle connection and controlling the eccentric phase on compound lifts.
Rest times: Compound lifts — 2-3 min. Isolation/accessory work — 60-90s. Don't rush the big lifts; partial recovery reduces both the weight you lift and the stimulus you get.
Weekly rotation: Your main compound lifts stay the same every week so you can track real progress. Accessory exercises rotate week to week to keep things fresh — this is intentional, not random.
Progressive overload: The Last Session column shows what you logged last time. Aim to add a small amount of weight, or an extra rep or two, each time.
